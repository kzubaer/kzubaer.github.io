Research Summary

Non-covalent supramolecular polymers are tunable, reversible, and stimuli-responsive, making them appealing for soft materials, self-healing systems, and nanotechnology. Bis-urea-based supramolecular polymers have perfect columnar stacking and guided hydrogen bonding. However, precise polymer length and dispersity modulation remains difficult. Monofunctional chain capper agents can control these systems' size and properties. These end-capping molecules inhibit polymer development by interrupting self-assembly. The dynamic equilibrium and shape of supramolecular constructs can be controlled by altering capper concentration and configuration. Diverse monofunctional chain cappers are added to bis-urea systems to study their effects on polymer length, stability, and assembly behavior. This discovery may aid the systematic design of tailored supramolecular materials.

We are investigating ways to construct accurate nanoscale ensembles of three to 150 molecules. To bridge the gap between single molecules and supramolecular polymers, 1–50 nanometer ‘molecular rulers’ are being created. Attaching large groups on the macrocycles' exteriors, using chain-stopping monomers to stop nanotube growth, and adding guests and templates to stabilize stacks of specific size are our ways to control the size of these nanotubular assemblies. The precise control of assemblies this tiny could alter our understanding of chemical, light-driven, and electron transport processes at these length scales.
